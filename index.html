Entrepreneurship (ENTR) – Integrated Overview

Minor in Entrepreneurship • HKUST Undergraduate Curriculum

The Entrepreneurship (ENTR) curriculum at HKUST supports a mindset–fundamentals–skillset progression for students who want to discover opportunities, build ventures, and engage with entrepreneurial ecosystems.

Entrepreneurship Minor Structure

The Minor in Entrepreneurship is open to undergraduates meeting the CGA and minor requirements.

Students complete at least 18 credits within a three-layer scaffold: one required mindset course, a set of fundamental electives, and skill-set electives that build practical entrepreneurial capabilities.

How the Entrepreneurship Minor Fits Together

Every Entrepreneurship Minor student shares the same starting point (ENTR 1001), then chooses fundamentals and skill-set courses to create a pathway that fits their interests.

ENTR Course Descriptions (2025–26)

This section consolidates ENTR-coded courses listed in the 2025–26 undergraduate course catalog together with the Entrepreneurship Minor documentation, focusing on course purpose and intended learning outcomes at a summary level.

ENTR 1001 – Entrepreneurship 1001: Designing Your Future

ENTR 1001 is a common core, experiential course where students from any discipline work in teams to design, launch, and grow a simple online business while reflecting on their own future paths as innovators and entrepreneurs.

The assessment structure is deliberately designed to walk students through the full entrepreneurial cycle: forming teams, understanding customers, building and testing offerings, communicating value, and consolidating personal learning.

  • Team formation and participation: attendance, in-class activities, and a team-building workshop ensure students commit to their team, practice collaboration under uncertainty, and build core teamwork habits needed for entrepreneurial projects.
  • Customer and market understanding: business model and market research tasks (such as customer journey mapping, funnels, competitor analysis, and customer discovery assignments) train students to see problems through users’ eyes and to structure opportunities using tools like the Business Model Canvas.
  • Running an online business: shop set‑up, marketing assignments, and growth experiments require each team to operate an actual or simulated online venture, make basic pricing and positioning decisions, and learn from real customer responses.
  • Prototyping and testing: prototype assignments (software, hardware, or service mock‑ups) and simple user tests develop students’ design thinking skills—building, showing, gathering feedback, and refining their solutions based on evidence.
  • Communication and pitching: midterm and final presentations (often supported by decks, videos, or campaign pages) help students practice structuring a compelling story about their idea, progress, and learning for a mixed audience of instructors, peers, and guest entrepreneurs.
  • Reflection and personal development: speaker reflections, teamwork reflections, and a personal development journal connect course activities back to students’ own strengths, values, and career design, reinforcing ENTR 1001 as a “designing your future” experience rather than a purely business exercise.
  • Peer evaluation and feedback culture: structured peer evaluation at key milestones encourages students to give and receive constructive feedback, recognize contributions within the team, and internalize professional norms around accountability and collaboration.

ENTR 3013 – Prototyping Skills for Entrepreneurs

ENTR 3013 is a hands-on, lab-based course that teaches students how to turn entrepreneurial ideas into tangible prototypes across physical and embedded-system domains. The course emphasizes rapid, iterative prototyping as a way to explore concepts, communicate with stakeholders, and de‑risk early venture ideas.

Students are introduced to key prototyping principles, then select and complete two focused five‑week skill-set modules (one in physical prototyping and one in embedded systems) before applying what they have learned in an integrated final project that showcases their prototype and communicates its value.

  • Enhanced learning outcomes: by the end of the course, students will be able to:
    • Explain why prototyping is critical in concept development, communication, and product development cycles, and distinguish between different types of prototypes and their purposes.
    • Plan and execute iterative prototyping cycles that move from rough concepts to increasingly refined representations based on user and stakeholder feedback.
    • Select appropriate tools, materials, and technologies (e.g., 3D CAD, 3D printing, embedded processors, sensors) to realize a prototype that demonstrates key features of an entrepreneurial idea.
    • Integrate hardware and/or embedded systems into prototypes where relevant, including basic electronics, input/output control, and simple IoT connections.
    • Communicate a prototype’s value proposition effectively through demonstrations, presentations, and documentation tailored to potential users, partners, or investors.
    • Collaborate in small teams to scope, build, test, and present a prototype that could feed into more advanced venture-building courses or competitions.
  • Course flow by week:
    • Weeks 1–2 – Prototyping foundations: introduction to rapid prototyping, why it matters for entrepreneurs, types of prototypes (low‑fidelity vs. high‑fidelity), and how iterative prototyping links to product development.
    • Weeks 3–7 – Skill-set Module I (Physical Prototyping): students dive into digital modeling and physical fabrication:
      • Basic 3D concepts and 3D CAD tools; Boolean operations and constructive solid geometry.
      • Free‑form modeling and scratch modeling using CAD software.
      • 3D printing principles, methods, materials, and limitations.
      • Post‑processing and complementary tools such as laser cutters and water‑jet cutters for 2.5D work.
    • Weeks 8–12 – Skill-set Module II (Embedded System Prototyping): students build foundational embedded and IoT skills:
      • Introduction to embedded systems and basic electronics; where and why embedded processors are used.
      • Embedded processor hardware, basic digital logic, and simple computing structures.
      • Embedded software concepts and programming models for simple applications.
      • Input/output control and interfacing sensors and actuators with embedded processors.
      • Basic IoT ideas: sensor types, actuation, and connecting devices to gather data or trigger actions.
    • Week 13 – Integrated project execution and presentation: teams use the skills from both modules to develop and present a working prototype (physical and/or embedded), accompanied by a short explanation of the concept, target users, and next steps.
  • Assessment focus (indicative): assessment can include individual lab participation and checkpoint tasks (e.g., CAD exercises, simple embedded programs), module mini‑projects, and a final team prototype plus presentation. The balance is designed to reward both technical skill development and the ability to use prototypes to tell a clear entrepreneurial story.
  • Position in the ENTR pathway: ENTR 3013 is an ideal follow‑on to ENTR 1001 and ENTR 3100 for students who have identified promising ideas or sectors and now need tangible prototypes. It also pairs naturally with ENTR 3012 (growth) and ENTR 3360/ENTR 490x for students who want to move from “prototype” to “startup”.

ENTR 3100 – Industrial & Deep Tech Landscape: Understanding the Elements to Start a Business

ENTR 3100 acquaints students with the landscapes of selected industries by examining the elements required to start a business and the structure of key markets within those sectors. Students explore how companies, technologies, regulations, and customers interact in an industrial ecosystem, and how this interaction creates both constraints and openings for new ventures.

In each offering, the course focuses on one or two thematic sectors (for example, aging and the silver economy, AI‑enabled services, robotics in logistics or healthcare, green and climate‑tech solutions, or biotech applications). Within these sectors, students engage with enabling technologies, key processes, business models, regulatory issues, and case studies of both incumbents and startups. They then translate this sector understanding into concrete venture opportunity spaces, including those based on deep tech or university research.

The course is jointly run by the Schools of Engineering, Science, and Business & Management, and may involve external partners (such as banks, technology firms, or deep‑tech labs) who co‑design project briefs and interact with student teams. This joint delivery ensures that students see how scientific discovery, engineering capabilities, and business models come together inside real industrial and entrepreneurial ecosystems.

  • Enhanced learning outcomes: by the end of the course, students will be able to:
    • Analyze interaction and power dynamics among different companies and stakeholders in an industry, including incumbents, startups, regulators, customers, and technology providers.
    • Understand the competitiveness, value chains, and business models of industrial and deep‑tech sectors, including how technology trends reshape these structures over time.
    • Develop empathy toward specific ecosystems and user groups (for example, aging populations, industrial clients, patients, climate‑stressed communities) through interviews, site visits, and data collection.
    • Identify and articulate venture opportunities in a chosen sector, including opportunities that leverage deep technologies such as AI, robotics, biotech, or climate‑tech and could be developed further in capstone or acceleration courses.
    • Perform effective teamwork and communication in cross‑disciplinary groups, including co‑creating ecosystem maps, opportunity briefs, and sector‑focused pitches.
    • Present clear, evidence‑based “sector venture briefs” that connect ecosystem analysis, user needs, enabling technologies, and potential business models.
  • Sector focus per term: to provide depth as well as breadth, each semester emphasizes one or two focal areas such as:
    • Aging & Silver Economy (e.g., healthtech, elder‑care robotics, fintech for seniors).
    • AI‑enabled services (e.g., AI in education, financial services, logistics, or creative industries).
    • Robotics & automation (e.g., warehouse automation, service robots, industrial robotics).
    • Climate‑tech and green solutions (e.g., energy, mobility, circular economy, smart buildings).
    • Biotech and life‑science applications (e.g., diagnostics, med‑tech, agri‑tech).
    Students are assigned or self‑select into cross‑disciplinary teams aligned with these sectors so that technical, business, and social perspectives are represented.
  • Core learning activities: the course is structured around a sequence of linked activities:
    • Industry and ecosystem mapping: teams map key actors, value flows, regulations, technologies, and pain points in their chosen sector, producing visual ecosystem maps and short analytical write‑ups.
    • Deep dives into enabling technologies: sessions led by disciplinary faculty or invited experts introduce sector‑relevant technologies (for example, AI models, sensor systems, bioprocesses, or climate‑monitoring tools) and their commercialization pathways.
    • Guest talks and panels: industry partners, deep‑tech labs, and startup founders share cases on how they navigate their ecosystem, which students later reference in their analyses and opportunity briefs.
    • Customer and stakeholder interviews: teams identify and interview potential users, customers, or stakeholders in their sector to validate pain points and refine their ecosystem view.
    • Opportunity‑framing assignments: students produce problem/opportunity statements, preliminary business models, and simple “what‑if” scenarios that connect ecosystem insights to candidate venture ideas.
    • Final sector venture brief and pitch: each team delivers a concise written brief and a pitch that describe their ecosystem, a specific opportunity space, proposed solution directions, enabling technologies involved, and possible next steps (for example, moving into ENTR 3360 or ENTR 490x).
  • Assessment focus (indicative): assessment can include individual participation and reflections (on sector talks, site visits, and user interviews), group ecosystem maps and analysis reports, opportunity‑framing assignments, and the final sector venture brief and team presentation. This mix ensures that both rigorous sector analysis and entrepreneurial creativity are recognized.
  • Position in the ENTR pathway: ENTR 3100 serves as a bridge between mindset courses (such as ENTR 1001) and more execution‑oriented courses (such as ENTR 3012, ENTR 3013, ENTR 3360, and ENTR 490x). Teams that identify promising opportunities can carry these ideas forward into prototyping, growth, and acceleration courses.
  • Staffing model: a core ENTR instructor coordinates the course and overall assessment, while sector‑specific faculty and external partners provide targeted inputs (guest lectures, project briefs, feedback). This structure makes it possible to run different sector themes in different semesters without redesigning the entire course each time.

ENTR 4911 – IT Entrepreneurship

ENTR 4911 (alternate code COMP 4911) focuses on the process of starting and growing information technology ventures, from recognizing an opportunity and shaping an “unfair” advantage to planning for funding and eventual exit. The course sits at the intersection of technology, business, and law, using real startup and corporate cases from Hong Kong and other ecosystems.

Students explore what makes IT ventures different from conventional businesses, how to design robust business models and plans, and how to navigate funding stages, intellectual property, and governance issues. The course uses a mix of lectures, case discussions, and a substantial team project to help students experience what it takes to move an IT startup from concept toward execution.

  • Enhanced learning outcomes: by the end of the course, students will be able to:
    • Identify the key principles and practices involved in founding or joining an IT startup and recognize common pitfalls that lead to failure.
    • Summarize core concepts in IT entrepreneurship, including opportunity evaluation, value proposition design, business models, funding mechanisms, and exit options.
    • Recognize and act on opportunities to build both an enterprise and a personal business network within local and global tech ecosystems.
    • Evaluate potential IT business opportunities and make reasoned recommendations about whether and how to pursue them.
  • Typical topics covered:
    • Entrepreneurial mindset in IT: opportunity recognition, unfair advantage, and founder–market fit.
    • SWOT and competitive analysis for tech ventures; business models and business plan writing for software, platforms, and digital products.
    • Technology, intellectual property, and corporate structures relevant to IT startups; the role of angels, venture capital, and other funding sources from seed to IPO.
    • Market and ecosystem analysis for key regions such as Hong Kong, mainland China, and global tech hubs; comparison of different startup environments.
    • Case studies of successful and failed IT ventures, including large tech firms and local startups, often supported by guest talks from practitioners.
  • Assessment focus (indicative):
    • Class participation and contribution to discussions and case analyses.
    • Group project in which teams develop a business plan for an IT venture and deliver midterm and final pitches or presentations.
    • A written exam or in-class test to check understanding of core concepts, plus short write-ups on talks, seminars, or competitions where appropriate.
  • Position in the ENTR pathway: ENTR 4911 is best suited for students who already have some exposure to entrepreneurship (e.g., through ENTR 1001, ENTR 3100, ENTR 3012, or ENTR 3360) and want a focused capstone-style experience in IT and digital ventures. It complements technical majors by showing how to turn computing skills into viable startup opportunities and complements the Entrepreneurship Minor by adding a strong IT-specialized layer.

ENTR 2010 – Entrepreneurship Mentorship and Readings

ENTR 2010 combines mentorship and curated readings to expose students to entrepreneurship in a flexible, interest-driven way, graded on a pass/fail basis.

  • Students engage with a mentor, choose focus areas to study, and participate in entrepreneurship activities; experiences are later consolidated with the instructor.
  • Key outcomes include understanding success and failure factors of startups, common entrepreneurial terminology, risks and benefits of entrepreneurship, and fit with personal career goals.

ENTR 3012 – Tech Startup and Entrepreneurs Ecosystem: Growth and Innovation

ENTR 3012 provides hands-on experience in developing and implementing growth strategies for early-stage technology startups and digital services, emphasizing startup ecosystems and practitioner engagement.

  • Students apply tools and frameworks such as the SAVE model and Blue Ocean ideas to analyze markets, craft value propositions, design growth campaigns, and develop investor pitches.
  • Assessment typically includes in-class and preparatory work, a major project, and attainment of digital analytics certifications that support data-driven experimentation.

ENTR 3030 – Social Innovations and Entrepreneurship

ENTR 3030 addresses how social enterprises and commercial entities can deliver social impact beyond what traditional government or charity models can easily achieve.

  • Topics include social enterprise objectives and business models, CSR, sustainability, team building, case studies, and strategies for social innovation, often supported by site visits and projects.
  • Students learn to identify social issues and explore models and operations that create measurable social value.

ENTR 3350 – Global Product Development

ENTR 3350 (also ISDN 3350) is a joint lecture and project-based course in which global, interdisciplinary teams identify problems, design solutions, and build prototypes with partner institutions in multiple cities.

  • Students perform market research, apply design tools and systems engineering, and collaborate internationally across online and face-to-face sessions.
  • They learn to articulate customer needs, design global products, collaborate in diverse teams, and present outcomes in public exhibitions.

ENTR 3360 – From Product Innovations to Successful Technology Startups

ENTR 3360 (also ISDN 3360) targets students who wish to seriously pursue technology startups, building from existing project ideas and prototypes toward more complete venture concepts.

  • Lecture and mentoring topics span market research, industrial design, IP, supply chains, business models, and funding and incubation opportunities.
  • Students analyze successful case studies, explore business opportunities from technology innovation, and connect with manufacturing and sales networks.

ENTR 4000 – Special Topics in Entrepreneurship

ENTR 4000 covers selected emerging or hot topics in entrepreneurship that are not yet part of regular ENTR offerings.

  • The course may be graded using different schemes, and students can repeat it for credit when topics differ.
  • Across offerings, it aims to strengthen entrepreneurial mindsets and the ability to create or increase value in organizations.

ENTR 4901–4904 – Student-led Entrepreneurship Acceleration Project

ENTR 4901–4904 are project courses where small student teams accelerate an entrepreneurial endeavor with guidance from a faculty advisor.

  • Activities include ideation, prototyping, operations planning, experiments, market surveys, user feedback, business planning, soft-launches, and pivoting.
  • Students synthesize technical, business, and user insights to evaluate venture survivability and societal impact, and build personal and professional networks.
